## ACT 1: POST-SEGREGATION NAMING (0:30-2:00)

**[VISUAL: Timeline graphic - 1964 Civil Rights Act to 1975]**

**VOICEOVER:**
1964: The Civil Rights Act passes. Segregation becomes illegal. And a generation of Black parents faces a new question: What do we name our children in this new America?

**[VISUAL: Historical footage - integration, Black Power movement]**

The 1970s brought choices. You could assimilate—choose names like Jennifer, Michael, Michelle. Or you could claim identity—choose names that signaled Blackness, African heritage, cultural pride.

**[VISUAL: Show Jamal chart - flat until 1975, then explosive growth]**

Jamal. Arabic for "beauty." In 1975, only 8 babies were named Jamal. By 1980, that number hit 130. By 1994, it peaked at 1,230. A 154× increase.

**[VISUAL: Compare to other names from same era - Malik, Kareem]**

Malik. Arabic for "king." Kareem. Arabic for "generous." These weren't tied to single celebrities—they were cultural movements. Post-segregation Black parents choosing names that rejected European colonialism and embraced African, Arabic, or created African-American identities.

**[VISUAL: Nation of Islam influence, Black Power imagery]**

Influenced by the Nation of Islam (Muhammad Ali, Malcolm X), by Black Power (Kwame Nkrumah, Stokely Carmichael), by a generation saying: "We don't need white approval anymore."

**[VISUAL: Pattern graphic - names that grew independently]**

These are Black Classics: names that became established in the Black community post-1975, grew steadily for 15-20 years, and weren't dependent on any single celebrity.

## ACT 2: THE GOLDEN AGE (1975-1995) (2:00-4:00)

**[VISUAL: Montage of 1980s-1990s Black culture - music, fashion, pride]**

**VOICEOVER:**
The golden age of Black Classics ran from about 1975 to 1995. This was when names like Ebony, Tanisha, Keisha, Latoya for girls, and Jamal, Darius, Rashad, Malik for boys became standard in Black communities.

**[VISUAL: Ebony magazine covers through decades]**

Ebony. The name peaked at 2,279 babies in 1982. Directly tied to Ebony magazine, yes, but also to the concept—Blackness as beautiful, valuable, worthy of celebration.

**[VISUAL: Show Tanisha chart - sustained popularity 1975-1990]**

Tanisha. Peaked at 758 in 1982. Not tied to a celebrity. Just a name that felt right to a generation of Black parents. African-sounding, distinctive, proud.

**[VISUAL: La Toya Jackson contrast]**

La Toya is the exception that proves the rule. It peaked at 5,051 babies in 1984—clearly tied to La Toya Jackson's fame. But watch what happens when her career fades.

**[VISUAL: Chart shows dramatic drop]**

By 1999, only 367 babies were named Latoya. By 2005, just 53. The celebrity-tied name died with the celebrity's relevance.

**[VISUAL: Compare to Jamal - which stayed strong]**

But Jamal? Still 1,492 babies in 2012. Still 907 in 2019. It stayed strong because it wasn't dependent on one person. It was a generation's choice.

**[VISUAL: List of Black Classics that sustained]**

Growing Classics (still popular in 2000s):
- Jamal: 1,230 peak (1994) → 907 (2019)
- Malik: 1,659 peak (2002) → still strong
- Kareem: Actually grew, peaked 382 (2019)

Stable Classics (consistent):
- Ebony: Moderate but persistent
- Rashad: Stayed in 300s range

**[VISUAL: Transition to decline]**

But not all Classics survived the generational shift.

## ACT 3: THE FADE (1995-2020) (4:00-6:00)

**[VISUAL: Charts showing dramatic declines - Tamika, Keisha, Latoya]**

**VOICEOVER:**
What parents chose in 1975 is not what their children chose in 2005. And nowhere is this more visible than in the Declining Classics.

**[VISUAL: Tamika chart - from 2,160 (1975) to 37 (2005)]**

Tamika. Peaked at 2,160 babies in 1975. By 2005, only 37. A 98% decline.

**[VISUAL: Keisha chart - similar dramatic drop]**

Keisha. Peaked at 1,051 in 1977. By 2005, only 97. Down 91%.

**[VISUAL: Why did these fade? Analysis graphic]**

Why? What happened?

**[VISUAL: Split screen - 1980s Baby Boomers vs 2000s Millennials]**

Generational taste shift. The parents of the 1970s were Baby Boomers and early Gen X—first generation post-segregation, asserting Black identity through names. Their children, millennials naming babies in the 2000s, had different contexts.

**[VISUAL: Different cultural moments - 2000s vs 1980s]**

By the 2000s, Blackness was mainstream in different ways. Hip-hop had gone global. Barack Obama would soon be president. The names that felt revolutionary in 1975 felt dated in 2005.

**[VISUAL: Comparison - what replaced them]**

What replaced Tamika and Keisha? 
- More gender-neutral names (Jordan, Kennedy)
- Arabic names with celebrity ties (Aaliyah, Khadijah)
- Unique, invented names (Zion, Nasir, Amari)
- Classical names (Olivia, Sophia, Isabella crossing racial boundaries)

**[VISUAL: Pattern graphic showing cycles]**

Naming patterns are generational. What defines you is what you reject from your parents. The Millennials didn't need to assert Black identity the same way their parents did. They grew up with it as baseline.

## ACT 4: KAREEM - THE EXCEPTION (6:00-7:15)

**[VISUAL: Kareem Abdul-Jabbar footage - basketball legend]**

**VOICEOVER:**
But one Black Classic defies the pattern: Kareem.

**[VISUAL: Kareem chart showing steady growth through 2019]**

Named for Kareem Abdul-Jabbar (born Lew Alcindor), who converted to Islam in 1971 and changed his name. You'd expect the name to peak in the 1970s-1980s during his playing career, then fade.

**[VISUAL: Animate chart showing opposite pattern]**

Wrong. The name grew slowly through the 1980s-1990s, then accelerated in the 2000s-2010s. It peaked at 382 babies in 2019—nearly 50 years after Kareem's conversion.

**[VISUAL: Why? Multiple factors]**

Why?

**ONE:** The name transcended the individual. "Kareem" became a Black Classic independent of the basketball player. It meant "generous" in Arabic—a virtue, not just a person.

**[VISUAL: Muslim community growth]**

**TWO:** Growing Black Muslim community. As Islam grew in Black communities, Arabic names gained legitimacy beyond celebrity association.

**[VISUAL: Other Kareems - politicians, artists, community leaders]**

**THREE:** Multiple reference points. By 2019, there were many prominent Kareems—not just Abdul-Jabbar. The name had diversified.

**[VISUAL: Pattern lesson]**

The lesson: Names that transcend their celebrity origins can outlast their era. Names that become about meaning, not just about a famous person, become truly classic.

## ACT 5: WHAT MAKES A CLASSIC? (7:15-8:30)

**[VISUAL: All charts from episode side by side]**

**VOICEOVER:**
So after analyzing 60 years of data, what makes a name a "Black Classic"?

**[VISUAL: Three criteria appear]**

**ONE:** Post-segregation establishment (1975+). These names emerged after civil rights, when Black parents had real choices.

**TWO:** Community adoption, not celebrity-driven. Multiple families choosing independently, creating critical mass without a single famous bearer—or transcending their famous bearer.

**THREE:** Generational resonance. They meant something specific to their generation—identity, pride, resistance, beauty.

**[VISUAL: Show three outcomes]**

And Black Classics have three possible fates:

**Growing:** Still relevant (Jamal, Malik, Kareem) - They transcended their era.

**Stable:** Consistent but moderate (Ebony, Rashad) - They found their level.

**Declining:** Faded with their generation (Tamika, Keisha, Latoya) - They were tied too closely to one era's aesthetics.

**[VISUAL: Comparison to celebrity names from earlier episodes]**

The difference between Black Classics and celebrity names? Celebrity names are about legitimizing one name through fame. Black Classics are about a generation claiming legitimacy for themselves, en masse.

**[VISUAL: Return to 1975 thesis]**

1975 parents saying: "We can name our children whatever we want. Jamal. Keisha. Ebony. These are beautiful. These are ours."
