The Exception That Defied the Pattern

Expected: The name "Kareem" should have peaked in the 1970s-1980s during Kareem Abdul-Jabbar's playing career, then faded.

Reality: It peaked at 459 in 1980, yes. But then it stayed strong—300-350 babies per year through the 1990s-2000s. By 2019, nearly 50 years after his conversion, it hit 382 babies. That's 83% of its historic peak, half a century later.

Why? Three reasons:

1. The name transcended the individual. "Kareem" became a Black Classic independent of the basketball player. It means "generous" in Arabic—a virtue, not just a person.

2. Growing Black Muslim community. As Islam grew in Black communities, Arabic names gained legitimacy beyond celebrity.

3. Multiple reference points. By 2019, there were many prominent Kareems—not just Abdul-Jabbar. The name diversified.

The lesson: Names that transcend their celebrity origins—names that become about meaning, community, identity—outlast their era. They become truly classic.
