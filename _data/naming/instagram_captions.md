### CAPTION

471×

That's how much the name "Aaliyah" increased from its baseline—the highest increase factor we found for any mainstream name.

But here's the twist: Aaliyah's name exploded AFTER her death.

2001: Aaliyah dies in a plane crash at age 22

Her death didn't end her influence—it intensified it. Documentaries, tributes, canonical status in R&B history.

The lesson: It's not just about being alive and famous. It's about cultural legitimacy. Once a name is tied to someone truly iconic, that legitimacy can outlast the person.

Zora Neale Hurston died in 1960, largely forgotten.

Fast forward to 2019: 349 babies named Zora
A 32× increase from pre-2000.

Why? The book became CANONICAL. High school AP English. College courses. Community book clubs.

If you're Black and educated in America after 2000, you likely read Zora.

This is posthumous legitimacy—40+ years after death, vindication through curriculum.

## STANDALONE POST: THE THREE BUCKETS

### CAPTION

After analyzing 170+ names and 60 years of data, we discovered three distinct patterns:

**1. MAINSTREAM TAKEOVER**
Large numbers (5,000+ babies)
Crossover to all demographics
Music & sports dominate
Examples: Mariah (5,446), Whitney (9,536), Shaquille

**2. BLACK AUTHORITY**
Moderate numbers (500-1,600 babies)
Legitimacy WITHIN Black community
TV sitcoms & authors dominate
Examples: Khadijah (1,615), Tatyana (1,161), Colson (654)

**3. BLACK CLASSICS**
Sustained over decades
Not celebrity-driven
Generational identity markers
Examples: Jamal, Malik, Ebony, Keisha
