## ACT 1: THE THESIS (0:30-2:00)

**[VISUAL: B-roll of 1980s-1990s MTV, album covers, award shows]**

**VOICEOVER:**
For decades, Black parents faced an impossible choice: give your child a "white-sounding" name to help them navigate a discriminatory world, or give them a name that honors their heritage and risk the consequences.

**[VISUAL: Academic studies, resume screening statistics]**

Studies have shown that résumés with "Black-sounding" names get fewer callbacks than identical résumés with "white-sounding" names. Names carry assumptions. Names carry risk.

But something shifted in the 1980s and 1990s. When Black celebrities achieved a certain level of fame—not just famous, but inescapable—their names shifted too. What was once coded as "too ethnic" became aspirational. Mainstream. Safe.

**[VISUAL: Transition to data visualization intro]**

I analyzed 60 years of Social Security baby name data—from 1960 to 2019—to prove this isn't just anecdotal. The numbers tell a clear story: when Black celebrities cross over into mainstream dominance, their names follow. And musicians do this better than anyone.

**[VISUAL: Whitney chart reveal - name label appears]**

That spike? That's Whitney Houston.

## ACT 2: WHITNEY HOUSTON - THE CLEAREST PROOF (2:00-4:30)

**[VISUAL: Full Whitney chart, 1960-2019]**

**VOICEOVER:**
Before 1985, "Whitney" existed—but it was relatively rare. Averaging around 600 babies a year. Not a name that carried strong cultural associations in either direction.

**[VISUAL: Chart highlights pre-1985 flat line]**

Then Whitney Houston released her debut album. And everything changed.

**[VISUAL: Animate the 1985-1986 spike slowly]**

1985: 3,833 babies named Whitney. Already a jump. But 1986—the year of her second album, the year she became genuinely inescapable—9,536 babies. In a single year. That is the largest single-year peak in our entire dataset for any name tied to a Black celebrity.

**[VISUAL: Put 9,536 in context - show scale vs. other names]**

To understand what 9,536 means: that's not a Black naming trend. Black Americans represent roughly 13% of the population. If only Black families were choosing Whitney, the number would be in the hundreds. Nearly ten thousand means white families. Asian families. Latino families. Everyone.

**[VISUAL: Highlight the sustained plateau 1986-1993]**

And it wasn't just a spike. The name stayed elevated for nearly a decade—through the Bodyguard era, through I Will Always Love You, through her Super Bowl performance. Parents weren't just reacting to a moment. They were watching a legacy build in real time.

**[VISUAL: Show the post-1993 decline]**

Then it fades. Not because Whitney stopped being talented—but because the cultural saturation faded. The era moved on. And here's something that surprised me in the data:

**[VISUAL: Zoom into 2011-2013]**

When Whitney Houston passed away in February 2012, I expected a posthumous spike. The Aaliyah effect. But it didn't come. The name continued its slow decline—521 in 2012, 482 in 2013.

Why? Because Whitney's name had already done its cultural work. It peaked WITH the moment. The name "Whitney" was so tied to a specific era that by 2012, it felt vintage rather than fresh. Compare that to Aaliyah, who we'll get to in a moment—a name that was still rising when she died, that had cultural momentum left to spend.

**[VISUAL: Side-by-side Whitney vs. Aaliyah trajectory preview]**

Timing, it turns out, matters as much as fame.

## ACT 3: AALIYAH - LEGITIMACY OUTLASTS A LIFETIME (4:30-6:30)

**[VISUAL: Aaliyah chart, 1960-2019]**

**VOICEOVER:**
Aaliyah is the most dramatic story in the dataset.

**[VISUAL: Highlight pre-2001 numbers - small but growing]**

Before 2001, the name was growing steadily. 1,451 babies in 1994 when her debut album dropped. It had cultural momentum—but it was a community-level name. Not a mainstream one yet.

**[VISUAL: Mark 2001 on chart with a vertical line]**

In August 2001, Aaliyah died in a plane crash at 22 years old.

**[VISUAL: Animate the post-2001 rise slowly]**

And then the name grew. Not despite her death—because of what her death revealed about her cultural standing. Documentaries. Tributes. Her music being sampled and cited and canonized by a generation of artists who grew up listening to her.

**[VISUAL: Highlight 2012 peak - 5,510]**

By 2012—eleven years after her death—5,510 babies were named Aaliyah. That is a 141-times increase from her pre-fame baseline. The highest increase factor we found for any mainstream name in 60 years of data.

**[VISUAL: Compare scale to Whitney]**

Whitney's peak was larger in raw numbers. But Aaliyah's increase factor is unmatched. Because "Aaliyah" started from near zero. It was a name that didn't exist in mainstream consciousness before she made it one.

**[VISUAL: Show the name sustaining post-peak]**

And unlike Whitney, Aaliyah's name didn't collapse after peak. It's been declining gradually—but it's still over 3,600 babies a year in 2019. The name has taken root. It's no longer just Aaliyah's name. It's a name.

**[VISUAL: Side-by-side Whitney decline vs. Aaliyah sustained]**

That's the difference between a name that peaks with an era and a name that becomes part of the culture permanently.

## ACT 4: MARIAH CAREY - AND A COMPLICATION (6:30-8:00)

**[VISUAL: Mariah chart, 1960-2019]**

**VOICEOVER:**
Now I want to show you a third chart. And I want to be honest with you about why it's more complicated.

**[VISUAL: Highlight Mariah's 1996 peak - 5,446]**

Mariah Carey. Peak of 5,446 babies in 1996. A 25-times increase from her pre-fame baseline. By the raw numbers, this fits our thesis perfectly.

**[VISUAL: Pause on chart]**

But here's the question I kept coming back to: is Mariah Carey a Black celebrity?

**[VISUAL: Hold. Let that land.]**

She's biracial—Black and Venezuelan on her father's side, Irish-American on her mother's. And when she debuted in 1990, she was largely presented and marketed as racially ambiguous. MTV-era Mariah wasn't coded as a Black artist the way Whitney was. The debate about her identity has followed her career—and it's still alive today.

**[VISUAL: Compare Mariah and Whitney charts side by side]**

So what does her naming data actually tell us? A few things worth sitting with.

One: her peak is massive—but it comes six years after debut. Whitney's spike was immediate, tied directly to her album. Mariah's climb was slower, more sustained. That lag might reflect how her identity was processed differently.

Two: the scale is comparable to Whitney's. Which means families of all backgrounds were choosing it. Whether they saw Mariah as Black, biracial, or racially ambiguous—they chose the name.

**[VISUAL: Text on screen: "Does racial ambiguity make a name MORE accessible to white families?"]**

Three—and this is the question I don't have a clean answer to yet: did Mariah's ambiguity actually accelerate the mainstream adoption? Did white families feel more comfortable choosing a name associated with someone they didn't fully read as Black?

That's a harder question. And it deserves its own episode.

**[VISUAL: Brief tease - "Coming: The Ambiguity Effect"]**

For now: Mariah fits the pattern. But she complicates it too. And that complication might be the most interesting data point of all.

## ACT 5: THE PATTERN (8:00-9:00)

**[VISUAL: All three charts side by side - Whitney, Aaliyah, Mariah]**

**VOICEOVER:**
Three musicians. Three different paths to the same outcome.

Whitney: immediate, massive, era-defining. The name peaked with the moment and declined with it.

Aaliyah: slower build, then a posthumous surge that outlasted her. The name is now permanently part of the culture.

Mariah: sustained climb, massive peak—but complicated by questions of identity that the data alone can't answer.

**[VISUAL: Key characteristics appear as text]**

What they share: all three hit numbers too large to be explained by Black families alone. All three crossed over. All three made names that were once considered too ethnic, too other, too different—acceptable. Desirable. American.

**[VISUAL: Pull back to thesis statement]**

Fame is a legitimacy machine. And in America, legitimacy has always been racialized. These charts aren't just about names. They're about which people—and which cultures—get to be considered mainstream. And when.
