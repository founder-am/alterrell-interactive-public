## OPENING SECTION

### Hook Paragraph
In 1990, only 218 babies were named Mariah. Six years later, that number hit 5,446—a **25-times increase**. This isn't random. This is what happens when an "ethnic" name meets mainstream fame.

For the next five weeks, I'm publishing a data-driven investigation into how Black celebrity transforms American baby names. We analyzed 60 years of Social Security data and found patterns that prove a simple thesis:

> **An "ethnic" name is ghetto... until you're famous. Then it's accepted. Then it's popular.**

This is Episode 1: Mainstream Takeover.

## SECTION 1: THE DATA

### Header: 📊 Mariah Carey: The Pattern Revealed

**[EMBED: mariah_short_v2.html OR inline image]**

Before 1990, the name "Mariah" averaged just 218 babies per year. It existed, but it was rare. Distinctive. Ethnic-coded.

Then a 20-year-old singer with a five-octave range released her debut album. **Mariah Carey** went multi-platinum. She wasn't just famous in Black communities—she was MTV, pop radio, crossover famous.

Watch what happens from 1990 to 1996. The name climbs steadily. Parents are watching. They're waiting. They're seeing Mariah Carey win Grammys, dominate charts, become a global superstar.

**In 1996: 5,446 babies named Mariah.**

## SECTION 2: SCALE

### Header: 🎤 Whitney Houston: The Largest Peak

**[EMBED: whitney_houston.html OR inline image]**

If Mariah shows the pattern, Whitney Houston shows the **scale**.

In 1986, during her peak superstardom with the album "Whitney," **9,536 babies** were named Whitney. That's the largest single-year peak in our entire dataset for names tied to Black celebrities.

Before 1985, "Whitney" averaged around 1,700 babies per year. During her peak years, it **quintupled**. This isn't a trend—it's a cultural tsunami.

### Pull Quote
> "Whitney Houston didn't just cross over—she dominated. Pop charts, R&B charts, movie soundtracks, the Super Bowl. She was America's voice. And parents responded by giving their daughters her name at unprecedented rates."

## SECTION 3: THE TWIST

### Header: 💔 Aaliyah: The Posthumous Effect

**[EMBED: aaliyah.html OR inline image]**

Perhaps most striking is **Aaliyah**.

In 2001, R&B singer Aaliyah died in a plane crash at age 22. She was already famous—albums, movies, a distinctive sound. But watch what happens to her name **after** her death.

The name doesn't fade. It **explodes**.

From 145 babies in 1994 to **5,510 in 2012**. A 471-times increase from baseline—the highest increase factor we found for any mainstream name.

Aaliyah's death transformed her into a legend. Documentaries, tributes, sampling in hip-hop. Her music became canonical. And parents—of all backgrounds—responded by naming daughters after her in numbers that dwarfed her lifetime popularity.

## SECTION 4: THE PATTERN

### Header: 🎯 What Mainstream Takeover Means

Three characteristics define this pattern:

**1. Large absolute numbers**  
Thousands of babies per year. That only happens when you reach all demographics—not just Black families, but everyone.

**2. Sustained visibility**  
Parents wait to see if the fame is real. Mariah's spike took 6 years to peak. Whitney's built over three albums. They evaluate before naming.

**3. Cultural saturation**  
These artists weren't just famous—they were **inescapable**. MTV, radio, movies, award shows. Everywhere.

Music is the ultimate mainstream takeover vehicle because it crosses every boundary. You don't need to speak English to appreciate Mariah's voice. You don't need to share someone's race to connect with Whitney's power.

**When musicians become that famous, their names transcend too.**

### Summary Paragraph
From 218 babies to 5,446. That's what happens when an ethnic name meets mainstream fame. Mariah Carey didn't just succeed as an artist—she **legitimized a name** that might otherwise have been dismissed as "too ethnic," "too different," "too other."

Whitney Houston took it further with the largest peak ever recorded. Aaliyah proved the effect can even grow after death. These aren't just naming trends—they're measurements of when America decides a name is acceptable.
