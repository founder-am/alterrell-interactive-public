The Curriculum Effect

"I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings" was published in 1970. Unlike celebrity spikes that peak quickly, Maya Angelou's name grew steadily for 36 years—the longest sustained growth in our dataset.

This isn't a spike. It's accumulation of cultural authority. Every year, more students read her work in classrooms. More parents internalize: "Maya" represents wisdom, resilience, beauty, survival. The name compounds generation after generation.

2006 peak (5,047 babies) came two years after receiving the Presidential Medal of Freedom. By then, Maya Angelou wasn't just an author—she was an institution. Her name had become canonical, required reading, part of the American literary landscape.
