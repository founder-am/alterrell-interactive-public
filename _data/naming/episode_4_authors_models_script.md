## ACT 1: THE CURRICULUM EFFECT (0:30-1:45)

**[VISUAL: School textbooks, Required Reading lists, college syllabi]**

**VOICEOVER:**
Authors have a unique power: they become required reading. Once a book enters the curriculum—high school English classes, college syllabi, book clubs—it reaches millions of young people forming their identities. And those young people grow up to become parents.

**[VISUAL: Show Maya Angelou chart - sustained growth 1970-2006]**

Maya Angelou. "I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings" was published in 1970. The name "Maya" grew steadily for 36 years, peaking at 5,047 babies in 2006, two years after she received the Presidential Medal of Freedom.

**[VISUAL: Highlight the slow, sustained climb vs. sudden spikes]**

This isn't a spike—it's a decades-long accumulation of cultural authority. Every year, more students read her work. Every year, more future parents internalize: "Maya" represents wisdom, resilience, beauty.

**[VISUAL: Transition to thesis]**

But the 2010s showed us something new: how contemporary literary acclaim creates naming trends in real-time. And it starts with two Pulitzer Prizes.

---

## ACT 2: COLSON WHITEHEAD - THE PULITZER EFFECT (1:45-4:00)

**[VISUAL: Colson Whitehead chart - focus on 2016-2019]**

**VOICEOVER:**
Before 2016, the name "Colson" barely existed. An average of 8.7 babies per year. It was essentially a non-name.

**[VISUAL: Chart shows flat line until 2016]**

Then Colson Whitehead published "The Underground Railroad." The book becomes a phenomenon—Oprah's Book Club, National Book Award, and in 2017, the Pulitzer Prize for Fiction.

**[VISUAL: Animate spike to 322 in 2017]**

Watch what happens: 322 babies named Colson in 2017. That's a 37-fold increase in a single year.

**[VISUAL: B-roll of "The Underground Railroad" book, reviews, acclaim]**

But Whitehead wasn't done. In 2019, he published "The Nickel Boys"—another devastating historical novel about a reform school in Jim Crow Florida. And in 2020, he won his second Pulitzer Prize.

**[VISUAL: Spike continues to 654 in 2019]**

2019: 654 babies named Colson. A 75× increase from his pre-fame baseline.

**[VISUAL: Show the pattern - two spikes aligned with two Pulitzers]**

This is the Pulitzer Effect: when America's most prestigious literary award validates a Black author not once but twice, parents respond. The name transforms from nonexistent to aspirational in three years.

**[VISUAL: Context - what Colson represents]**

Think about what choosing "Colson" means in 2019. You're naming your child after a Black intellectual who writes about slavery and racism with unflinching honesty. You're making a statement: "We value this. We honor this. We are this."

That's cultural authority.

---

## ACT 3: ZORA NEALE HURSTON - THE CANON REVIVAL (4:00-5:30)

**[VISUAL: Zora Neale Hurston chart - focus on 2014-2019]**

**VOICEOVER:**
Sometimes literary legitimacy comes decades after an author's death. Zora Neale Hurston died in 1960, largely forgotten. Her masterpiece "Their Eyes Were Watching God" was out of print.

**[VISUAL: Historical footage/photos of Zora Neale Hurston]**

Then in 1975, Alice Walker published an essay: "In Search of Zora Neale Hurston." She literally searched for Hurston's grave in an overgrown Florida cemetery and marked it with a headstone. The essay sparked a revival.

**[VISUAL: Chart shows very low numbers through 1990s-2000s]**

The name "Zora" stayed rare through the 1990s and 2000s. An average of 11 babies per year pre-2000. But watch the 2010s.

**[VISUAL: Animate growth from 2014-2019]**

From 139 babies in 2014 to 349 in 2019. A 32× increase. Why?

**[VISUAL: B-roll of high school curricula, AP English, college courses]**

"Their Eyes Were Watching God" became canonical. Not just assigned in colleges—assigned in high schools. AP English. Community book clubs. If you're Black and educated in America after 2000, you likely read Zora.

**[VISUAL: 2018 event - Barracoon published posthumously]**

2018: Barracoon, Hurston's final book, published posthumously after sitting in archives for 87 years. More media attention. More conversations about her genius.

**[VISUAL: Chart reaches 349 peak]**

2019: Peak year. 349 babies named Zora.

**[VISUAL: Pattern comparison - Colson vs Zora]**

The difference? Colson's spike is contemporary acclaim. Zora's spike is posthumous canonization. But both represent the same thing: when literature validates a name, parents listen.

## ACT 4: TYRA BANKS - THE SUPERMODEL EXCEPTION (5:30-6:45)

**[VISUAL: Transition to fashion - Tyra Banks on runway]**

**VOICEOVER:**
Models occupy a strange space. They're famous, but not for their work—for their appearance. And historically, modeling hasn't created large naming spikes. Naomi, Iman, even Alek Wek—all had moderate impacts.

**[VISUAL: Show Tyra Banks chart]**

But Tyra Banks broke the mold. The name "Tyra" peaked at 943 babies in 1998—a 5.9× increase from baseline.

**[VISUAL: Tyra's career milestones - Sports Illustrated, Victoria's Secret, Paris runways]**

Why did Tyra work when other models didn't? Because she didn't stay in fashion.

**[VISUAL: 2003 - America's Next Top Model debuts]**

2003: "America's Next Top Model" debuts. Suddenly Tyra isn't just a model—she's a TV personality, a mentor, a businesswoman. She's in millions of homes every week, just like the sitcom stars we discussed in Episode 3.

**[VISUAL: Show sustained popularity through 2000s]**

The name stays popular through the 2000s because Tyra stays visible. She diversifies. She becomes more than her body—she becomes a brand, a voice, an authority.

**[VISUAL: Compare to male models - Tyrese Gibson chart briefly]**

Male models face an even harder path. Tyrese Gibson—model, singer, actor—saw his name increase 51×, but only after crossing into music and Fast & Furious films. Modeling alone wasn't enough.

**[VISUAL: Pattern summary]**

The lesson: Models need crossover. Beauty isn't enough to legitimize a name. You need sustained cultural presence, television, or entrepreneurship.

---

## ACT 5: THE EDUCATION EFFECT (6:45-8:15)

**[VISUAL: Montage of all authors/models discussed]**

**VOICEOVER:**
So what separates authors and models from musicians and actors?

**[VISUAL: Three key differences appear]**

**ONE:** Intimacy through education. When you read someone's words—really read them, analyze them, write essays about them—that creates a different connection than hearing a song or watching a show. Reading is personal. Curriculum is mandatory.

**TWO:** Class signaling. Naming your child after a Pulitzer winner signals something different than naming them after a pop star. It says: "We value intellect. We value literature. We are the educated Black middle class."

**[VISUAL: Show the "Black Authority" pattern from Episode 3]**

This is Black Authority in a different form. Not sitcom authority (community and representation), but literary authority (education and achievement).

**THREE:** Long-tail legitimacy. Maya Angelou's name grew for 36 years. Zora's revival took 40+ years. These aren't flash-in-the-pan trends. When a name is tied to canonical literature, it has staying power.

**[VISUAL: Arab names callback - Colson, Zora alongside Khadijah, Aaliyah]**

And notice: Many of these names—Colson, Zora, Tyra—sit alongside the Arab names we discussed. They all represent distinct identities, cultural pride, resistance to assimilation.

**[VISUAL: Statistics comparison]**

The numbers are smaller than musicians (Colson 654 vs Mariah 5,446), but the meaning is different. These aren't mainstream crossover names. They're Black middle-class names. Parents choosing them are making a statement about values, education, identity.
