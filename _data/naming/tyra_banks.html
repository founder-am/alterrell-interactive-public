Why Tyra Worked When Other Models Didn't

Most supermodels don't create large naming spikes. Naomi Campbell, Iman, even Alek Wek had moderate impacts. Models are famous for their appearance, not their work—and that doesn't typically legitimize names at scale.

But Tyra Banks diversified. She didn't stay in fashion. Sports Illustrated (1996, 1997), Victoria's Secret, international runways—that built visibility. But the peak (943 babies in 1998) came as she was transitioning into business, TV hosting, and building her brand.

By 2003, she launched "America's Next Top Model," putting her in millions of homes weekly—just like sitcom stars. She became a personality, mentor, businesswoman. The name stayed popular through the 2000s because Tyra stayed visible beyond the runway.

The lesson: Beauty isn't enough. Models need crossover—television, entrepreneurship, sustained cultural presence—to legitimize their names at scale.
