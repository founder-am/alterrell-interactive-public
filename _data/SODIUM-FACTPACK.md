## SECTION 2 — THE ARGUMENT IN ONE SENTENCE

Fast food chains systematically load their most affordable menu items — the ones low-income and food-insecure communities rely on most — with sodium levels that exceed daily recommended limits in a single meal, and this is a structural choice, not an accident of flavor.

**Two anchor claims (everything orbits these):**
1. The cheapest items carry the highest sodium loads — affordability and health risk are inversely correlated by design
2. Proximity and price make fast food the de facto grocery store for food-insecure zip codes — sodium overload is not a choice, it is an infrastructure outcome

## SECTION 3 — VERIFIED SODIUM DATA

### FDA / AHA daily limits
- **FDA recommended daily sodium intake:** 2,300 mg
- **AHA ideal ceiling (heart health):** 1,500 mg/day
- **Average American daily intake:** ~3,400 mg (CDC)
- Source: FDA, AHA, CDC dietary guidelines

## SECTION 4 — STRUCTURAL FRAMING: THE FOOD ACCESS ARGUMENT

### Food desert geography
- **USDA definition of food desert:** Low-income census tract where a substantial number of residents have low access to a supermarket or large grocery store
- **Approximately 19 million Americans** live in food deserts (USDA Food Access Research Atlas)
- Fast food density in food-insecure zip codes is measurably higher than in food-secure zip codes
- Source: USDA Food Access Research Atlas

### The affordability trap
- Dollar menu and value tier items are disproportionately high in sodium relative to cost-per-calorie
- Families in food-insecure households are not making bad choices — they are making the only choices available at price points that fit their budgets
- This is the "Data as Dignity" editorial frame: the data reveals infrastructure failure, not individual failure

### Health cost downstream
- Hypertension affects approximately **47% of American adults** (CDC, 2023)
- Sodium is one of the most directly modifiable dietary drivers of hypertension
- Communities with highest fast food reliance overlap significantly with communities with highest hypertension rates
- Source: CDC / AHA cardiovascular data

## SECTION 5 — RUDD CENTER DATA (PRIMARY SOURCE)

- Rudd Center for Food Policy and Health (UConn) is the primary academic source for fast food advertising and nutrition tracking
- Rudd Center data documents: sodium levels across chains, advertising spend targeting low-income and minority communities, and children's menu nutrition profiles
- **Key Rudd Center finding relevant to this piece:** Fast food advertising is disproportionately targeted at Black and Latino youth — the same communities that face the highest food insecurity rates
- Source: Rudd Center published reports (specific report year/title to be confirmed in editorial session before next activation)

---

## SECTION 6 — ADVERTISING TARGETING DATA

- Fast food chains spend heavily on advertising in low-income and minority media markets
- Black youth are targeted at significantly higher rates than white youth for fast food advertising despite equal or greater health risk
- Rudd Center documented McDonald's, Burger King, Taco Bell, and Popeyes as among highest spenders in targeted minority advertising
- **This is the secondary structural argument:** The product is designed for affordability, optimized for addiction (salt/fat/sugar), and then specifically marketed to the communities with the least access to alternatives
- Source: Rudd Center advertising reports

## SECTION 7 — EDITORIAL FRAMING DECISIONS (LOCKED)

- **"Data as Dignity" application:** Never frame as "people are eating badly." Frame as "the infrastructure is designed to deliver harm at scale."
- **No individual judgment.** The data is about systems, not choices.
- **Primary audience emotional arc:** Recognition ("I didn't know that was that much sodium") → Systemic reframe ("Wait, the cheap stuff is always the highest?") → Structural outrage ("This is by design") → Agency ("Here's what the architecture looks like")
- **CBUXO note:** Non-analytical readers must be able to feel the argument before they can follow the data. The visual entry point matters more than the table.
