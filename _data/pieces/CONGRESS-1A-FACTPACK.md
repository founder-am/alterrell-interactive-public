## SECTION 2 — THE ARGUMENT IN ONE SENTENCE

The U.S. Congress is populated by people whose median age, wealth, and career tenure are structurally misaligned with the constituents they represent — and the electoral system that keeps them there is not a bug, it is a feature that incumbents have built and maintained.

**Two anchor claims (everything orbits these):**
1. The age, wealth, and tenure gap between members of Congress and their constituents is not incidental — it is the predictable outcome of structural advantages that incumbents have legislated for themselves
2. The "Senator Selfish" frame: Congress members are not failing their constituents through incompetence — many are succeeding at a different job entirely (self-perpetuation)

## SECTION 3 — THE AGE GAP: VERIFIED DATA

### Congressional age vs. constituent age
- **Median age of U.S. House members (119th Congress, 2025):** approximately 57–58 years
- **Median age of U.S. Senate (119th Congress, 2025):** approximately 64–65 years
- **Median age of U.S. adult population:** approximately 38–39 years
- **Gap:** Senate median is approximately 25–27 years older than the median American adult
- Source: Congressional Research Service / CRS reports on congressional demographics; U.S. Census

### The oldest members (documented)
- Senate has historically included members in their 70s, 80s, and in recent terms, members showing documented cognitive decline in public settings
- This is not an argument about individual capacity — it is an argument about median representation
- Source: CRS, C-SPAN, congressional records

### The primary race exception (locked)
**Moulton vs. Markey (Massachusetts Senate) — the one primary race in scope**
- Seth Moulton: age ~47 (born 1978)
- Ed Markey: age ~79 (born 1946)
- Age gap: 32 years — the largest documented age gap in any current primary race with a 20+ year differential
- This is the only primary race the piece covers — the age gap is structural evidence, not endorsement
- **Editorial rule:** The piece does not endorse candidates. It documents structural patterns. Moulton/Markey is cited as the clearest single example of the age gap argument made literal in a ballot choice.
- Source: FEC filings, public records

## SECTION 4 — THE WEALTH GAP: VERIFIED DATA

### Congressional wealth vs. constituent wealth
- **Median net worth of U.S. House member:** approximately $1M–$1.5M (varies by reporting cycle)
- **Median net worth of U.S. Senate member:** approximately $3M–$4M
- **Median net worth of U.S. household:** approximately $192,700 (Federal Reserve, 2022 Survey of Consumer Finances)
- **Gap:** Senate median net worth is approximately 15–20x the median American household
- Source: OpenSecrets congressional wealth data; Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances

### Stock trading and conflicts of interest
- The STOCK Act (2012) requires members to disclose trades within 45 days
- Documented violations: hundreds of members have filed late disclosures; enforcement is minimal
- The academic literature documents that congressional stock portfolios historically outperformed the market — the "congressional advantage" study
- Source: OpenSecrets STOCK Act tracker; academic research on congressional trading performance

### The self-dealing argument
- Members who have served for 20+ years have accumulated wealth and network advantages that are structurally tied to their continued service
- Term limits polling: consistently 70%+ of Americans support congressional term limits across party lines — this is one of the rare bipartisan public opinion findings
- Congress has never voted to impose term limits on itself
- Source: Gallup polling on term limits; congressional voting records

## SECTION 5 — THE TENURE ARGUMENT

### How long they stay
- **Average House tenure:** approximately 8–10 years (4–5 terms)
- **Average Senate tenure:** approximately 11–12 years (approaching 2 terms)
- **Longest-serving members:** multiple members with 30–40+ years of service documented
- Source: CRS reports on congressional tenure

### Incumbent advantage mechanics
- **Franking privilege:** Members can send official mail at government expense — a de facto campaign communication advantage not available to challengers
- **Name recognition:** Incumbents benefit from years of constituent communication, press coverage, and official event attendance funded by office budgets
- **Fundraising network:** Long-tenured members have accumulated donor networks that are not accessible to first-time challengers
- **Redistricting:** Members often have influence over the drawing of their own district lines through the legislative process — documented in states without independent redistricting commissions
- Source: Brennan Center for Justice; FEC fundraising data; redistricting documentation by state

### Reelection rates
- **House incumbents who run for reelection win approximately 90–95%** of the time in modern elections
- **Senate incumbents:** approximately 80–85% reelection rate
- This is not evidence of public satisfaction — it is evidence of structural advantage
- Source: OpenSecrets incumbency data; CRS

## SECTION 6 — THE "DISTRICT-74" FRAME

### What the title means
- "District-74" is not a real district number — it is a constructed frame
- The argument: your representative is representing a district that includes themselves, their donors, their stock portfolio, and their institutional interests — a phantom constituency that travels with them
- "District-74" signals the phantom constituency without targeting a specific member
- This protects the piece from being read as targeted political opposition research while naming the structural pattern

### The "Senator Selfish" argument
- The piece is not saying members of Congress are bad people
- It is saying the incentive structure of modern congressional service optimizes for self-perpetuation over constituent service
- A rational actor in the system behaves exactly as documented: accumulates wealth, avoids contested votes, raises money from interests with business before their committees, and runs for reelection indefinitely
- The system is working as designed — the question is: designed for whom?

## SECTION 8 — CBUXO FLAGS

- **Non-analytical reader risk (high):** Congressional data can feel abstract and distant. The entry point must be personal and immediate — not a chart of average tenure.
- **Suggested non-analytical entry point:** Name your own representative. Show their age, their wealth, their years in office, and then show the median for their constituents. The gap is the argument, made personal.
