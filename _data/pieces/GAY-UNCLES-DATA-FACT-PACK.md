## 5. BLACK COMMUNITIES HAVE DIFFERENT BODY NORMS — ACROSS GENDERS

**Source:** Feliciano, Robnett, Komaie. "Internet Daters' Body Type Preferences: Race-Ethnic and Gender Differences." Sex Roles, 2009. Sample: 5,810 Yahoo heterosexual dating profiles.

**Finding:** African-American and Latino men are significantly more likely than White men to prefer dates with thick or large bodies. White males are more likely to prefer thin and toned partners.

**Supporting finding:** Non-whites have greater body satisfaction and are less influenced by mainstream media body ideals than whites.

**Limitation:** Heterosexual dating profiles. Female body preferences. 2009 data. But the underlying cultural pattern — Black and Latino communities having broader body acceptance — is directionally relevant to Black gay men's dating markets.

## 6. OVERWEIGHT BLACK WOMEN ARE MORE LIKELY TO DATE (NOT LESS)

**Source:** PMC study, "The Role of Body Size in Mate Selection among African American Young Adults." 2015. Sample: 386 African American adolescents and young adults ages 16-21, Midwest and Southeast.

**Finding:** Overweight Black women were significantly more likely to casually date than women in the thinnest weight category.

**Argument:** In Black communities, larger body size does not carry the same dating penalty it does in white communities. While this study is about heterosexual women, the cultural pattern — size as neutral or positive rather than disqualifying — maps directionally to the experience of bigger Black gay men in Black-majority cities.

## 7. THE GENERATIONAL SHIFT — BODY NEUTRALITY OVER BODY POSITIVITY

**Source:** EduBirdie survey, 2025. Sample: 2,000 Gen Z respondents on body image attitudes.

**Finding:** 78% of Gen Z respondents say the level of body positivity has gone too far, with 27% calling it overhyped and performative. Gen Z is shifting toward "body neutrality" — respecting what your body does rather than celebrating or critiquing how it looks.

**Your observation (from TikTok comments, not survey data):**
- Millennial men tend to frame attraction as a specific preference: "I love big boys." This positions size as a niche — you're desired *despite* or *because of* a specific trait.
- Gen Z commenters didn't qualify it: "You're just sexy — all of you." No category. No exception. Just attraction.

**Argument:** The generation that grew up with body neutrality treats size differently than the generation that grew up with "no fats no fems." Millennials frame size as a preference (fetish-adjacent). Gen Z frames it as a trait (normalization). This maps to the broader Gen Z shift from body positivity ("I celebrate your body") to body neutrality ("your body just is").

**Limitation:** The EduBirdie data is general population, not gay men, not Black. Your TikTok observation is your own experience from comments. Frame honestly: "I don't have survey data on this for gay men specifically, but here's what I noticed in the comments, and the broader generational data supports the direction."

The bar, the brunch, the pool party, the private event. They aren't waiting for a seat at someone else's table.

**Limitation:** Only DC and ATL confirmed to source. The mechanism (income → infrastructure → body norm freedom) is AMA's thesis, supported directionally but not proven.

## 9. THE APPS AREN'T NEUTRAL (for copy, not a card)

**Jack'd:** Reportedly ~30% Black user base (founders have stated this — specific interview/source TBD). Profile-forward design. Dominates in ATL, Houston, DC.

**Grindr:** Largest overall user base. Grid layout rewards photos first. Body filters are a feature. Dominant in LA, NYC.

**Scruff:** Bear/otter/muscle subculture. Body-type tags built into profiles. Same parent company as Jack'd (Perry Street Software), different market positioning.

**Argument:** The platform you're on shapes who sees you. In cities where Jack'd dominates, the user base already skews Black and body-inclusive. In cities where Grindr dominates, the visual hierarchy rewards the lean/muscular standard.

**Limitation:** No published city-by-city market share data. App dominance claims are community observation.
