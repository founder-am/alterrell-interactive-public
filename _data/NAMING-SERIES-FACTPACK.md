## SECTION 2 — THE ARGUMENT (PART 1)

Black-coded names — names that signal Black identity through cultural association — receive statistically fewer callbacks in hiring than identical resumes with white-coded names. This is documented, replicated, and persistent. The name on the resume is doing discriminatory work before the interview begins.

**Two anchor claims:**
1. The callback gap is real, replicated, and large enough to constitute a structural barrier — not statistical noise
2. This is not about the name being "unprofessional" — it is about race-coding operating as a screening mechanism

---

## SECTION 3 — VERIFIED DATA: RESUME AUDIT STUDIES

### The foundational study
**Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004) — "Are Emily and Greg More Employable Than Lakisha and Jamal?"**
- Published: American Economic Review
- Method: Sent ~5,000 fictitious resumes in response to 1,300+ job ads in Boston and Chicago
- Finding: Resumes with white-sounding names received **50% more callbacks** than identical resumes with Black-sounding names
- White-sounding names used: Emily Walsh, Greg Baker (examples)
- Black-sounding names used: Lakisha Washington, Jamal Jones (examples)
- The gap persisted across industries and job levels
- Source: American Economic Review, 2004. One of the most cited studies in labor economics.

### Replication and extensions
- The Bertrand & Mullainathan finding has been replicated across multiple subsequent studies
- The callback gap has been documented in: retail, finance, law, healthcare, and technology sectors
- A 2021 meta-analysis found the gap has not narrowed meaningfully in 25 years of studies
- Source: Kline, Rose & Walters (2022) — "Systemic Discrimination Among Large U.S. Employers" (NBER) — documented race discrimination at a majority of large employers tested

### SSA data: name patterns by race
- Social Security Administration birth name data allows tracking of names by racial composition of birth cohort
- Names classified as "distinctively Black" in research literature are identified through SSA data cross-referenced with birth certificate race data (where recorded) and census demographic matching
- This is the methodology behind the Bertrand/Mullainathan name selection and subsequent studies
- Source: SSA annual birth name data

---

## SECTION 4 — THE STRUCTURAL ARGUMENT

### Name as race proxy
- Employers screening for "professionalism" are — whether consciously or not — screening for race
- "Professionalism" as a concept in hiring has documented racial and class dimensions
- The name functions as a first filter before any other information is considered

### The compounding effect
- The callback gap is the first documented barrier — it is not the only one
- Those who do get callbacks face documented bias in interview, salary negotiation, and promotion
- The name discrimination data represents the floor of the problem, not the ceiling

### What the data does NOT say
- It does not say parents are making a mistake by giving their children culturally Black names
- It says the labor market is discriminating — and that is the system's failure, not the family's
- This is the "Data as Dignity" frame applied directly: the data reveals structural discrimination, never judges the individuals named

## SECTION 2 — THE ARGUMENT (PART 2)

The hiring callback gap is not the only place a name does structural damage. Housing applications, healthcare quality, educational tracking, and criminal sentencing have all been studied for name-based or race-coded discrimination. The name follows you into every institution.

**Core frame:**
- If the labor market gap were the only documented effect, it could be explained as one industry's bias
- When the same pattern appears across hiring, housing, healthcare, and education, it is architecture — not anomaly

---

## SECTION 3 — VERIFIED DATA: CROSS-SECTOR DISCRIMINATION

### Housing
- Audit studies have documented that rental inquiries from applicants with Black-coded names receive fewer responses and are offered worse terms
- The Fair Housing Act prohibits this — the data documents it happening anyway
- Source: Urban Institute housing audit studies / HUD-funded research

### Healthcare
- Studies have documented differential pain treatment and diagnostic attention by perceived race
- Name-based race-coding has been examined as one mechanism through which race is inferred before the patient is seen
- Research gap: direct name-audit studies in healthcare are less common than in hiring — flag as a documented gap in methodology section
- Source: JAMA / NEJM published studies on racial bias in pain treatment

### Education
- Teacher expectation research documents differential grading and attention based on perceived race of student names
- Study: Figlio (2005) — "Names, expectations and the black-white test score gap" — found teachers had lower expectations for students with distinctively Black names
- Source: NBER Working Paper 11195 (Figlio, 2005)

### Criminal justice
- Name-based race inference has been examined in studies of bail setting, sentencing, and prosecutor charging decisions
- Research is more diffuse in this domain — not as clean as the hiring audit literature
- Flag as directional evidence, not primary claim
