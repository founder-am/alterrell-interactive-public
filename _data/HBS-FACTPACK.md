## SECTION 2 — THE ARGUMENT IN ONE SENTENCE

Harvard Business School's institutional wealth, endowment growth, and alumni network have compounded for over a century — and the access that wealth buys is distributed in ways that reflect and reinforce the same structural inequalities the school purports to study and solve.

**Working anchor claims (to be stress-tested in Type 1 session):**
1. HBS produces the people who run the institutions — understanding who gets in, and who gets funded once they're in, is structural analysis, not gossip
2. The endowment is a civic asset that receives public subsidy (tax exemption) — its growth and distribution are legitimate subjects of public accountability

**Editorial note:** AMA has personal stake and access by virtue of being an alum attending the reunion. This is an asset — first-person framing grounds the systemic critique in observed reality, which is the AMA voice standard. The piece should use that access without becoming a personal essay.

## SECTION 3 — POTENTIAL DATA ARCHITECTURE

*This section represents scoped possibilities — the Type 1 editorial session must lock the primary argument and data approach before research begins.*

### HBS endowment data
- Harvard University endowment: approximately $50B+ (one of the largest university endowments in the world)
- HBS is a component of Harvard's total endowment — HBS-specific endowment figures are published in HBS annual reports
- Endowment growth rate over 10, 20, 30 years
- Comparison to peer institutions: Wharton, Stanford GSB, Kellogg, Booth
- Source: Harvard/HBS annual reports, NACUBO endowment data

### Alumni wealth concentration
- HBS alumni are disproportionately represented in Fortune 500 CEO positions, private equity, and hedge fund leadership
- The question this raises: what is the relationship between HBS attendance and access to capital — and how is that access distributed along race, gender, and class lines?
- Forbes 400 representation: percentage of Forbes 400 with Harvard/HBS affiliation
- Source: Forbes, LinkedIn alumni data, Harvard alumni directory (public records)

### Demographic data
- HBS student body demographics over time: race, gender, first-generation student percentage
- This data is published in HBS annual admissions reporting
- The trajectory matters: has the school's stated DEI commitment moved the needle on who is in the room?
- Source: HBS published admissions data

### Alumni giving and access
- Annual fund participation rates
- Named gift data (publicly available — buildings, fellowships, programs)
- The question: who funds the institution's named infrastructure, and what does access to that infrastructure look like for students without family giving history?

## SECTION 4 — THE DIGITAL MUSEUM FRAME

*"Digital museum" is the production concept — not confirmed as the final format. This needs editorial lock.*

### What "digital museum" means as a format
- The piece functions as a curated exhibition rather than a linear essay
- Each "exhibit" is a data moment — a specific, named, visualized finding
- The audience walks through the architecture rather than being told about it
- This format suits the HBS subject because the institution itself is built on curation, prestige, and selective display

### Potential exhibit structure (working — not locked)
- **Exhibit 1:** The Endowment — what it is, how it grew, what it's worth per enrolled student
- **Exhibit 2:** The Room — who has been in it, historically and now
- **Exhibit 3:** The Network — where HBS alumni concentrate power
- **Exhibit 4:** The Gap — what access looks like for those without family giving history or pre-existing network
- **Exhibit 5:** The Question — what the institution's stated values say vs. what the data shows

## SECTION 5 — EDITORIAL FRAMING DECISIONS

- **Personal stake framing:** AMA attends the reunion and observes. This is not a confessional piece — it is an observed-reality-to-systemic-critique structure. The personal is the entry point, not the subject.
- **"Data as Dignity" application:** The data is about institutional systems, not individual alumni. No alum is named as a villain. The architecture is the subject.
- **Title register:** Must follow the tabloid/op-ed rule — cultural provocation or zany observation that stops scrolling. "HBS Has a Museum Now and It's Called the Endowment" is an example register, not a locked title.
- **Timing:** June reunion creates a natural hook and a specific, datable publication moment. The piece should land during or just after the reunion window.

## SECTION 6 — CBUXO FLAGS

- **Non-analytical reader risk (high):** Endowment data and alumni network analysis can read as insider content that requires existing familiarity with how elite institutions work. The entry point must be visceral and specific — not abstract.
- **Suggested non-analytical entry point:** Start with a single dollar figure that is impossible to contextualize without help. Then provide the context. The revelation is the hook.
- **Emotional arc:** Familiarity (you've heard of Harvard) → Specific recognition (you didn't know it was this big) → Structural reframe (this is not philanthropy, this is infrastructure) → Accountability question (who benefits and who doesn't)
