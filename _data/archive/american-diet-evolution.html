⚗ Key research

Published studies that support the hypothesis: eating "the same food" today produces different health outcomes than it did decades ago.

NIH inpatient trial — Hall et al. (2019)
Randomized controlled trialn=20, inpatient 4 weeks
Participants ate 508 extra calories/day on ultra-processed vs whole-food diets — even though both were matched for calories, macros, sugar, sodium, and fiber. They gained 0.9 kg in 2 weeks on UPF and lost 0.9 kg on whole food. This was the first causal proof that processing itself — not just nutrients — drives overeating. Published in Cell Metabolism.
NHANES 18-year trend — Juul et al. (2021)
n=40,937 adults2001–2018 longitudinal
UPF consumption rose from 53.5% to 57% of calories across all demographics. Minimally processed food dropped from 32.7% to 27.4%. The trend held regardless of income, education, or race. Published in American Journal of Clinical Nutrition.
Clinical Nutrition umbrella review (2024)
39 meta-analyses reviewed
Found "highly suggestive evidence" linking UPF consumption to overweight/obesity, plus associations with cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome, depression, and cancer.
GRAS loophole — Env. Working Group (2021)
Regulatory analysis
99% of 766 food chemicals introduced between 2000–2021 bypassed FDA review via GRAS self-affirmation. About 3,000 chemicals in the food supply have never been reviewed by the FDA. Companies can self-declare substances safe without even notifying the agency.
Pew Research / USDA dietary data
Population-level, 1970–2010
Average American consumed 2,481 calories/day in 2010 vs 2,057 in 1970 — a 23% increase. Nearly half of all calories come from just two groups: flour/grains and fats/oils. Fruit and vegetable share of calories dropped from 9.2% to 7.9%.
CDC NHANES — obesity timeline
National survey, 1960–2023
Adult obesity: 13% (1960) → 15% (1980) → 23% (1994) → 31% (2000) → 36% (2010) → 40% (2020). Severe obesity rose from under 1% to nearly 10%. Childhood obesity tripled since the 1970s. The acceleration aligns with the introduction of HFCS, trans fats, and the GRAS loophole.
