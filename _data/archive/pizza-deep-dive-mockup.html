The policy trail
How legislation shaped your pizza
Each policy decision nudged the food industry toward cheaper ingredients, longer shelf life, and less oversight.
1958
Food Additives Amendment
Creates the GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe) framework. At first, a panel of experts reviews substances. This becomes the loophole that swallows the rule.
1970–73
Farm Bills end production controls
USDA shifts from "grow what we need" to "grow as much as possible." Corn subsidies explode. This creates the glut of cheap corn that becomes HFCS, corn syrup solids, modified corn starch, maltodextrin — ingredients that will fill pizza dough, sauce, and cheese for decades.
1977
Sugar tariffs rise
Import quotas and tariffs make cane sugar expensive. Beverage and food manufacturers rush to HFCS as a cheaper alternative. By 1984, both Coca-Cola and Pepsi switch entirely.
1980s
Trans fat era / frozen food boom
Partially hydrogenated oils (trans fats) are cheap, shelf-stable, and give frozen pizza that "fresh" texture. They'll be in virtually every processed food until the 2010s.
1990
Nutrition Labeling Act
Mandatory nutrition labels. But ingredient lists are in tiny print, and "natural flavors" can mask dozens of chemical compounds without disclosure.
1996
"Freedom to Farm" Act
Removes remaining crop limits. Deepens corn/soy subsidies. Corn-derived ingredients become even cheaper.
1997
FDA loosens GRAS rules
Companies can now self-affirm new chemicals as "safe" without even notifying the FDA. 99% of 766 chemicals introduced 2000–2021 will bypass FDA review via this loophole.
2006
Trans fat labeling required
But the rule allows "0g trans fat" if under 0.5g per serving. Companies shrink serving sizes to hit the threshold.
2015–18
FDA bans partially hydrogenated oils
Trans fats finally removed — but replaced with palm oil and interesterified fats. Different chemistry, similar function.
2020s
MAHA / state-level bans begin
California bans Red No. 3, brominated vegetable oil, potassium bromate, propylparaben. Federal reform remains uncertain.

Same brand, different rules
US vs EU: same pizza, different product
American and European versions of the same branded products have different ingredient lists because the EU bans or restricts additives the US allows.
🇺🇸
United States
~10,000
approved food additives
GRAS self-affirmation: companies declare own chemicals safe. 99% of new chemicals bypass FDA review. Precautionary principle NOT used.
🇪🇺
European Union
411
approved food additives
Precautionary principle: uncertain safety = banned until proven safe. No self-affirmation. Every additive must be on the Union positive list.
Banned in EU, found in US pizza: Azodicarbonamide (dough conditioner), BHA/BHT (preservatives), TBHQ (petroleum-derived preservative), potassium bromate (flour treatment), several FD&C synthetic dyes. The same brands (e.g. Dr. Oetker) reformulate with simpler ingredients for EU markets.
