## TAB 2 — THE LAST NAME PROBLEM [IN PROGRESS]

- Other naming traditions point backward to family: Icelandic -dóttir/-son traces to the actual parent. Clan names trace to tribe and region. Surnames in most cultures carry genealogical infrastructure.
- Black American last names frequently trace backward to enslavers, plantations, the geography of bondage — Mills, Jefferson, Washington, Jackson. The last name is a locked door with someone else's name on it.
- Name approval laws: legal obstacles to non-Western or non-standard names have existed and persist. The mechanism of control evolved. [DATA? — confirm specific current examples before build]
- The last name could not be the site of reclamation because it was already occupied. This earns Tab 3.

## TAB 3 — SO THE FIRST NAME BECAME THE WORK [IN PROGRESS]

- When the last name is compromised, the only available site is the first name. Black American naming practices did not develop in a vacuum — they developed in response to a specific structural wound.
- The first name became the carrier of everything the last name couldn't hold: aspiration, cultural heritage, spiritual meaning, the statement of who this child is and what they are allowed to be.
- Muhammad Ali chose his name and refused to answer to the other one. That was not incidental. The name was the thing that could not be taken without consent. [NON-ANALYTICAL BRIDGE NEEDED — give the reader a moment here before moving to the series intro]
- The naming practices in Parts 1–11 are not celebrity chasing. They are a community practicing the only form of genealogical infrastructure it was permitted to build.
- It's only ghetto until you're famous. The data agrees. But the data also shows how long that takes, and how rarely the structural penalty disappears entirely, even then.

## TAB 4 — WHAT THE DATA CAN AND CANNOT TELL US [IN PROGRESS]

- The SSA records every name given to 5 or more babies per year. It records what was named. It cannot record why.
- The data shows a spike in 1986 corresponding to Whitney Houston's debut. It cannot confirm that any specific parent named their daughter Whitney because of Whitney Houston. That gap is honest. The series lives in it.
- The absence of intent data is not a limitation to work around — it is part of the argument. The gap between what records show and what they cannot show is itself a story about record-keeping, power, and whose choices get documented.
- Methodology: names given to fewer than 5 babies per year are invisible. Small, deliberate naming acts — potentially the most intentional ones — are undercounted. The data almost certainly underestimates the community naming signal.
- The data cutoff is 2020. Deliberate and locked. Post-2020 trends excluded to protect editorial integrity.
