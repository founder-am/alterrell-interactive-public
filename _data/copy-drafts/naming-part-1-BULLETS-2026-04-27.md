## TAB 1 — OVERVIEW [IN PROGRESS]

- [NON-ANALYTICAL BRIDGE NEEDED — do not open with "fame makes names safe." Open with a scene: a parent in 1986 choosing a name. The data is the record of what happened. Not the other way around.]
- Fame does not just make stars. It makes names safe to give your child.
- Two mechanisms at work: mainstream crossover and community authority. They look different in the numbers. They mean different things.
- This piece is not about celebrity. It is about what Black families decided — at the moment of naming — was worth carrying forward.
- The structural penalty on a name does not disappear when the name becomes famous. It gets overwhelmed by volume. It returns when the volume fades.

## TAB 2 — THE SYSTEM [IN PROGRESS]

- Mechanism 1 — Mainstream crossover: the famous bearer is so dominant that racial coding temporarily collapses. Both Black and white families adopt the name in large numbers. The fame overwhelms the signal.
- Mechanism 2 — Community authority: the name moves inside Black communities specifically, smaller absolute numbers but with clearer intention. The community claiming its own, with or without crossover.
- The racial penalty does not disappear during the famous period. At peak Whitney (9,536 babies), the name is statistically too common to read as coded. That is not progress — it is volume.
- As the famous period ends and the name ages, the penalty returns. Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004) use names from this era specifically. [DATA? — Confirm Lakisha peaked in this era before sentence is written. Quillian 2017 meta-analysis also cited in brief.]
- Mainstream crossover produces bigger numbers that fade. Community authority produces smaller numbers that say something more specific.

- Community authority: moves smaller, intentional, without crossover. The community decided. Mainstream America found out later, or not at all.

## TAB 4 — WHITNEY & MARIAH [IN PROGRESS]

- [NON-ANALYTICAL BRIDGE NEEDED — open with what it meant to hear Whitney Houston for the first time, in that year, at that cultural moment. Data is the record of what followed.]
- Whitney peaked at 9,536 in 1986. 1 in 26 Black baby girls. One per elementary school class. Mainstream crossover at total scale.
- Mariah peaked at 5,446 in 1996 — 1 in 46 Black baby girls. Stayed elevated for two decades. 25× baseline. Sustained, not spiked. Different pattern.
- The two-decade Mariah effect: sustained cultural dominance produces maintenance mode. The name holds at an elevated baseline as long as the fame holds.
- The comparison: 9,536 Whitney babies vs. 300 Latifah babies is not a gap in cultural impact. It is a gap in mechanism. Both show the community responding to something worth naming a child after.
- The "1 in X" frame is the argument. 1 in 26 says: every classroom had a Whitney. 1 in 800 says: the community made a specific, intentional choice that most of America never noticed.
