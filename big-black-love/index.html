Advice From Your
Thick Gay Uncles

What the data says about where bigger Black gay men are loved

There was a moment — not a revelation, just a noticing. I was in a city that wasn't mine, and something was different. Men were looking. Not in the way you hope someone will when you're trying to convince yourself you're fine. In the way that makes you realize you'd stopped expecting it.

I came home and couldn't explain it. My friends had explanations ready. Confidence. Energy. Putting yourself out there. All the things people say when they don't want to tell you the market has a structure and you're on the wrong side of it.

So I asked. I posted a poll on TikTok — where did you, a bigger Black man, find your Black partner? I expected a handful of responses. I got a flood. And the same cities kept appearing. Atlanta. Chicago. Houston. DC. Not randomly. Not because bigger Black gay men only exist there. Because something about those cities created conditions where Black love could find you.

This piece is what I found when I tried to understand why.

It is not a confidence guide. Your uncle is not here to tell you to love yourself harder. You already do. This is a geography guide. It's data from your community about where the market gets it right — and the socioeconomic story underneath that explains how those cities got there.

If you're a bigger Black man who has wondered whether what you're experiencing is in your head — it isn't. It's in the zip code.

My Story

I Wasn't Always This Size

That sentence matters for this piece because my vantage point is specific. I know what it felt like to move through gay Black spaces when my body fit closer to what those spaces reward. I got attention I didn't have to work for. I made assumptions about desire that turned out to be assumptions about access. I didn't know the difference yet.

When my body changed, the rules I'd internalized stopped working. Not all at once. Gradually, then obviously. The apps felt different. Certain rooms felt different. The baseline assumption I'd carried — that I was someone who got looked at — quietly stopped being true in most places.

I want to be honest about what that means for this piece. I am writing from the experience of someone who crossed over. I had access I didn't earn and didn't fully recognize until it was gone. Some of you reading this were never on the other side of that wall. You were socialized into bigger bodies from the beginning and never had the privilege I'm describing to lose. My experience is not more valid than yours. It is just a particular angle of vision — I can see both sides of the wall, which is useful for making the argument this piece makes.

The hearing world analogy is the one that helped me most. Someone who loses their hearing as an adult grieves differently than someone born deaf. They also see the hearing world's assumptions differently — from the outside, having once been inside. That's the vantage point I'm writing from. Not better. Just specific.

What the TikTok poll gave me was confirmation that what I was noticing was real. I had felt the difference between cities. I had felt Atlanta differently than I felt other places. I thought maybe it was circumstance, or luck, or the particular room I'd walked into. Then four hundred people told me where they'd found their person, and the same cities came back over and over, and I understood that what I'd felt was structural.

This piece is me trying to show you the structure.

Methodology & Sources

This piece draws on community-generated poll data from a TikTok post and overlays it with publicly available socioeconomic data. The poll is not a representative sample. Cities were reported voluntarily by respondents and reflect one week of responses. n=77 total responses. Percentages calculated from n=77.

  • Community poll data: TikTok (@alterrell), April 2026. n=77 voluntary responses. Cities reported by respondents identifying as bigger Black gay men who found their Black partner. Not statistically representative.
  • Black population by city: US Census Bureau, American Community Survey (ACS) 2023 5-year estimates.
  • Gay population estimates: Williams Institute, UCLA School of Law. LGBT adult population by metro area. Figures are directional estimates, not race-adjusted.
  • Scatter chart uses log scale on both axes to prevent white-space compression from population outliers (NYC/Brooklyn Black pop: 1.9M). Log scale is the correct default for any population scatter on this platform.
  • Gay bars excluded from data layer — not a reliable proxy for Black gay infrastructure. Jack’s market presence and CDC body size data pending source verification. See BLACK-GAY-GEOGRAPHY-BRIEF.md open questions.
  • Accent color #E8B923 is a placeholder for this piece, acceptable until the second Obsidian Futures piece ships.
