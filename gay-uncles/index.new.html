Overview

I know what it's like to be treated like a two in one city and a seven in another while looking exactly the same. That's why I believe dating has markets.

Whenever I start talking about market value and product-market fit as it relates to dating, people assume I'm overthinking it. Sometimes they think I'm reducing people to numbers or making some kind of eugenics argument. I'm not. I'm describing a pattern I've lived over and over again. Once you experience it yourself, it's difficult to unsee.

We make enormous life decisions based on cities. We move because of work, family, college, weather, cost of living, or simply because we've always wanted to live somewhere. We ask whether a city will make us happier professionally, financially, or culturally. We rarely ask whether it'll make us happier romantically.

People love to say that people are basically the same everywhere. I don't think that's true. Cities develop personalities. Their values become culture. Their nightlife, dating pools, social circles, and even dating apps organize around those local cultures. New York isn't New Jersey. Oakland isn't San Francisco. DC isn't Baltimore. The people who live there would be the first to tell you they're different. If cities shape so much else about our lives, why wouldn't they shape dating too?

I actually know what it's like to be differently desired in different places, where the product, which is me, and the market I'm in, the fit is highly variable.

My college years were like a four-part A/B test. Nine months at school, three months at an internship, looking the same all year, and treated so differently that the market clearly changed even though the product was the same.

That's why I think "there's someone out there for everybody" is well intentioned but incomplete.

Sometimes there is someone out there. They just might not be here.

We spend a lot of time asking people to change themselves. Sometimes the better question is whether they're trying to build a life in the wrong market.

I'm intentionally focusing on bigger Black gay men who want to date other Black men. Part of that is practical. Untangling body politics is already complicated enough without adding interracial dating dynamics and racial fetishization.

Part of it is personal. So much of our media assumes Black love always means Black plus someone else. I'm interested in the couples many of us grew up seeing and quietly hoped for ourselves. If that's not your story, I still think parts of this will resonate. The underlying question is bigger than body size. It's about whether different places value different people in different ways.

I'm approaching 40, so I'm thinking about this as someone who is an uncle. Take this the way you'd take advice from an uncle or a gay elder. Keep what makes sense, throw away what doesn't. It may not apply today, but it might apply in the future.

Before we go any further, let's assume people are content with the body they're in. This isn't an argument that losing weight is the answer, and it isn't an argument that anyone is entitled to love.

What I'm arguing is much simpler. Our dating experiences aren't evenly distributed, and sometimes the people closest to us don't realize how different those experiences can be because they haven't lived them.

I'm also relatively new to living in a larger body, so I know there are blind spots in what I'm about to say. If you've been bigger your whole life, your experience may be very different from mine. That's part of why I wanted to build this. I want the conversation just as much as I want to make the argument.

My Story

I Wasn't Always This Size

I've lived in Boston, New York, the Bay Area, and London, and I've traveled to 38 countries. My body has been bigger and smaller throughout that time. What I've learned is that attraction isn't just about who you are. It's also about where you are.

When I lived in Boston, people treated me like I was a two. In New York I might be treated like a seven. Same body, different market. I went to Istanbul three times, and two of those times men approached me on the street, saying they loved Black men and lived right down the block. Then I went to South Korea and no one messaged me on any of the apps. I was staying in a beautiful hotel on a consulting gig thinking the whole room was going to waste.

Online, when I post a body picture, always clothed, I get so much praise. So many people say they desire larger bodies. That does not necessarily translate to my real lived experience here in New York City.

I went on a date recently and the guy said, you're so handsome, you must go on so many dates. Wrong answer. That is not my reality. I'm still choosing from a place of scarcity rather than abundance, and choosing from scarcity has made me feel like maybe I'm not choosing my best options.

Sources

Methodology & Sources

This piece draws on community-generated poll data from a TikTok post (@alterrell) and overlays it with publicly available socioeconomic data. The poll is not a representative sample. Cities were reported voluntarily by respondents from the full comment thread. n≈100 strict partner mentions from approximately 85 unique commenters across 25+ cities. Cards show top 10-11 cities; remaining cities acknowledged in copy. Atlanta and Chicago are near-tied at the top; framing as “tied” is defensible given the methodology. Video: 4K+ views, 562 likes, 174 comments. Previous tally (n=77, April 26) superseded by full-thread count.

  1. Community poll data: TikTok (@alterrell), May 2026. n≈100 strict city-mentions from ~85 unique commenters. Cities reported voluntarily by respondents identifying as bigger Black gay men who found a Black partner. Not statistically representative.
  2. LGBT population by metro area: Conron, K.J., Luhur, W., & Goldberg, S.K. “Estimated number of US LGBT adults in Large Metropolitan Statistical Areas (MSA).” Williams Institute, UCLA, March 2021. Based on Gallup Daily Tracking Survey 2012-2017 applied to 2017 Census ACS estimates. All-LGBT (not gay men specifically, not race-adjusted). Key figures: NYC-Newark-Jersey City MSA 706,000 (4.5%); ATL-Sandy Springs-Alpharetta MSA 194,000 (4.6%). NYC/ATL ratio: 3.6×.
  3. Obesity prevalence by race and state: CDC BRFSS 2022-2024, “Prevalence of Obesity Based on Self-Reported Weight and Height Among Non-Hispanic Black Adults / Non-Hispanic White Adults, by State and Territory.” Three-year pooled data, self-reported BMI ≥30. State-level only (not city-level) — used as a geographic proxy for poll cities. PA and LA excluded (insufficient data). Card framing: “above-average BMI” not “obesity.” Point spread format: Black adult rate minus White adult rate.
  4. Black median household income: U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2019-2023 5-Year Estimates via Neilsberg Research (2023 inflation-adjusted dollars). Confirmed: DC $60,089; Atlanta (city proper) $47,937. US national Black median: $53,444. Houston, Chicago, NYC, LA: directional figures only — full Census pull recommended before deploy.
  5. Black population share by city (M-3): Census ACS 2019-2023. Detroit city ~77% Black; Atlanta city proper ~47% Black.
